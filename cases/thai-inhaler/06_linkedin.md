# LinkedIn post — case 1 (thai-inhaler)

Draft. Voice: practitioner, not vendor. ~290 words.

---

The cheapest way to launch a wellness brand is to spend $5k validating someone else's product first.

A founder messaged me on LinkedIn last week with a real question: how to differentiate a culturally-rooted aromatherapy brand in a saturated US category. They wanted "deep market research and marketing approaches."

I ran 16 Claude sub-agents on it — 10 viewpoints in parallel (CMO, customer, competitor, PR, cultural domain, risk, skeptic, CFO, futurist, data analyst), then a red-team review, then five deep dives (FDA claim language, credible-carrier playbook, channel break-even economics, differentiation map, creator-economy fit), then a final synthesis. 1.42 million tokens. 33 minutes wall-clock. 47,588 words written across 17 files.

The honest answer wasn't a marketing playbook. It was a phased validation plan with explicit kill-switches.

The reframe: spend $5k buying off-the-shelf wholesale product, applying custom packaging, and listing it on Amazon at three price points. Six weeks of cheap conversion data answer the wedge question better than $25–100k of custom inventory does. If the conversion rate at the target price doesn't clear 1.5%, the founder saved a year of bootstrap runway. If it does, the wedge earned the right to custom inventory.

The single recommendation worth quoting: "Look at the verb. Look at its object. If the verb acts on the product, scent, or ritual, you are cosmetic-safe under FDA. If it acts on the body, system, emotion, or named condition, it is a drug claim. Stop." That rule alone has killed more indie wellness brands than any competitor.
