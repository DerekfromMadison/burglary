# LinkedIn post — case 1 (thai-inhaler) v2

Draft. Voice: practitioner, not vendor. ~290 words.

Changes from v1: replaced the "1.5% conversion" framing (which inherited a median-calibrated threshold from the v1 deliverable) with the recalibrated kill threshold; softened the compute-stat sentence to read as cost-of-rigor rather than compute-bragging.

---

The cheapest way to launch a wellness brand is to spend $5k validating someone else's product first.

A founder messaged me on LinkedIn last week with a real question: how to differentiate a culturally-rooted aromatherapy brand in a saturated US category. They wanted "deep market research and marketing approaches."

I ran 18 Claude sub-agents on it — 10 viewpoints in parallel (CMO, customer, competitor, PR, cultural domain, risk, skeptic, CFO, futurist, data analyst), then a red-team review, then five deep dives (FDA claim language, credible-carrier playbook, channel break-even economics, differentiation map, creator-economy fit), then a final synthesis, then a second-pass red team that caught a calibration error in the first synthesis. The kind of analysis that's only economic at sub-$30 of compute.

The honest answer wasn't a marketing playbook. It was a phased validation plan with explicit kill-switches calibrated above industry median.

The reframe: spend $5–15k buying off-the-shelf wholesale product, applying custom packaging, and listing it on Amazon at three price points. Six weeks of cheap conversion data answer the wedge question better than $25–100k of custom inventory does. The bar isn't "did it convert" — it's "did it convert *above the indie-wellness median*," because that median is what the wedge thesis claims to beat. Conversion below 1% kills the wedge. Median performance (1.5%) is conditional, not green-lit. Above 2.5% earns the right to custom inventory.

The single recommendation worth quoting: "Look at the verb. Look at its object. If the verb acts on the product, scent, or ritual, you are cosmetic-safe under FDA. If it acts on the body, system, emotion, or named condition, it is a drug claim. Stop." That rule alone has killed more indie wellness brands than any competitor.
