# Wave-1: Core Messaging Architecture

The brand-voice spec the founder uses for everything. Compliant under the verb-object rule from `D_fda_language.md` (verb's object = product/scent/ritual/moment = safe; verb's object = body/system/condition = drug claim, dead). Tuned to Segment 1 (Maya — the Nervous-System Regulator) as primary, Segment 3 (Pim — the diaspora gatekeeper) as PR shield. Segment 2 (Jen / Functional User) buys what Segment 1 makes legible.

## 1. Brand promise (1 sentence)

**A one-second pocket ritual — a Thai herbal inhale that turns the next minute brighter.**

Verb-object check: "turns the next minute brighter" — the object is "the next minute," a moment-noun. Safe per D_fda_language §3 meta-rule and table row #15 ("a glimmer for your day"). The verb does not act on the body, the nervous system, or any condition. The promise is sensory, temporal, and ritual — exactly the three columns the cosmetic safe-harbor allows.

## 2. Hero claim / headline

**Hero (lead everywhere — site H1, deck cover, Amazon A+ banner, pitch one-liner):**

> **One inhale. One bright minute. A Thai pocket ritual.**

Three short beats. "Inhale" = the user's action on the product (sensory action), not a verb acting on the body. "Bright minute" = aesthetic noun (cf. row #15 "a glimmer for your day"). "Thai pocket ritual" = cultural anchor (cf. row #11 "Inspired by ya dom, a Thai pocket ritual"). Memorable, scannable, screenshottable, and zero column-C language.

**Runner-up (use as PDP subhead or LinkedIn intro):**

> **Your morning glimmer, in a tube.**

Lifted directly from the D_fda_language §4(i) safe-website-headline list. "Glimmer" is the trojan horse — polyvagal-adjacent without the words *polyvagal*, *nervous*, or *system*. Reads modern-wellness-fluent without crossing the verb-object line.

Rejected (and why):

- *"Reset your nervous system in one breath."* — column C, table row #30. Object of "reset" is the nervous system. Drug claim.
- *"Pocket calm."* — "calm" alone is fine; "pocket calm" makes calm a noun-state of the user. Risky enough to skip when better options exist.
- *"The 5-second exhale your day needs."* — "your day needs" makes the day a need-state. Soft-but-creep.

## 3. Three tagline candidates

### T1: **"One inhale. One bright minute."**

Minimum-viable hero. Looks great on a 9:16 end-card, on a tin, on a sticker. Rationale: rhythm carries memorability; "bright minute" stays on the moment (object of brightening = minute, not mood). Verb-object pass: action acts on the product/inhale; brightness modifies the minute. Use as default tagline below the wordmark.

### T2: **"Pocket Thailand. One breath at a time."**

Anchors heritage cleanly. The verb here is "anchor your day's pace via the act of breathing" — acts on cadence, not lungs/anxiety. Safe. Rationale: this is the tagline that earns the Pim/diaspora double-take; it sounds like someone who actually knows what ya dom is. Use on packaging back-panel, founder bio, retail one-sheet.

### T3 (most provocative — the one to A/B test): **"Smells stronger than Vicks. Designed like jewelry."**

Comparison-class pivot. Says out loud what the customer is already triangulating in her head — *this is the upgrade from Vicks, and it deserves to live in my purse, not my medicine cabinet.* Rationale: the death zone in `A_cmo.md` is $14–$18 (where the brand looks like premium-Vicks); this tagline forces the comparison to medicine-cabinet-Vicks (we beat on aesthetic, ritual, purse-legibility) instead of drugstore-Vicks (we lose on price). Generates the share-line for TikTok comments. Verb-object check: "smells stronger" — verb's object is the scent. "Designed like jewelry" — verb's object is the product. Both safe. Use on TikTok captions, the launch ad, and the "why we exist" deck slide. If it draws a snippy DM from a Vicks lawyer, the founder has already won the news cycle.

## 4. Voice / tone spec

**Voice principles:**

1. **Sensory before symbolic.** Name the smell, the moment, the place. Translate olfactory experience into language that paints a picture.
2. **Short. Specific. Concrete.** The customer skims. Three nouns beat a paragraph.
3. **Honest about what it is.** It's an inhaler. It smells. We sourced it. We don't promise medical anything.
4. **Quiet authority on Thai-ness.** Reference incumbents (Hong Thai, Poy-Sian, Peppermint Field) by name when it earns trust; never "elevate" or "discover" the category.
5. **No wellness Mad-Libs.** The "calm. restore. awaken." copy is dead.

### DO (write like this):

- "Eucalyptus, camphor, and a little smoky-sweet borneol. Think a quieter Vicks, with a forest in it."
- "Inspired by ya dom — the green tube your Thai friend's mom keeps in her bag."
- "Built with the Wong family's blend, going back since the 70s. We pay 4× FOB. We named them on the label."
- "One inhale before the meeting. One after. That's the whole pitch."
- "It's an aromatic ritual. Not a pill. We promise this smells like this and feels like that."
- "A breath of cool Thai mountain air." (D_fda_language row #9)
- "Your morning glimmer, in a tube." (D_fda_language row #2)

### DON'T (these kill the brand):

- *"Calms the nervous system."* — column C, row #30. Body-system S/F claim.
- *"Boosts focus and improves concentration."* — column C, row #31. Cognitive S/F claim, requires RCT.
- *"Helps you sleep better."* — column C, row #32. FDA aromatherapy guidance lists this verbatim.
- *"Relieves anxiety naturally."* — column C, row #29. Named condition.
- *"Therapeutic-grade essential oils."* — column C, row #24. Direct FDA red flag.
- *"Ancient Thai secret, finally elevated."* — appropriation tell. Lights up Persona C's group chat.
- *"Calm. Restore. Awaken."* — wellness Mad-Libs death.
- *"This product is not intended to diagnose, treat…"* — that's a *supplement* disclaimer; on a cosmetic it signals drug-claim intent (per D_fda_language §1 decision tree).

### Tone calibration by surface

| Surface | Register | Example seed |
|---|---|---|
| Founder personal (LinkedIn / IG bio) | Confident, slightly dry, specific | "We make a Thai herbal inhaler. One breath, one bright minute. Sourced with the Wong family in Chiang Rai." |
| Brand social (TikTok / IG feed) | Sensory + funny, low ego | "Smells like a forest after rain and a quieter Vicks." |
| PDP / Amazon listing | Specific, scannable, three-note structure | scent → moment → ingredient |
| Retail / wholesale deck | Quiet authority, traction-led | numbers, named supplier, named creators, no adjectives |
| Customer service | Plain English, kind, never defensive | "Yep — camphor's the same compound that's in Vicks, at aromatherapy levels. It's an inhaler, not a medicine." |

## 6. Success metric — what proves the messaging is working

Three signals, in priority order:

1. **Verbatim resharing.** "One inhale, one bright minute" or "smells stronger than Vicks, designed like jewelry" shows up in customer captions and comments unprompted within 60 days. **Target: ≥20 organic uses of brand-coined phrases by day 60.** This is the only metric that proves language is sticky enough to compound; everything else is paid amplification.
2. **TikTok save-rate / share-rate, not view count.** Saves and shares are the real intent signal for "I want to act on this later" (Persona A's screenshot pattern). **Target: ≥4% save-rate on top-quartile organic posts; ≥1.5% share-rate.** View counts are vanity once the algorithm finds the audience.
3. **Cold-Amazon CTR.** Listing CTR from cold Sponsored Products. **Target: ≥0.45% CTR (Amazon Beauty median ~0.35%); ≥1.5% conversion at $14 single SKU** (per E_final Phase 0 gate). If headline language cannot beat Amazon median CTR by 25%+, the title and the first PDP image need rewriting.

Secondary watch-list: founder LinkedIn first-week impressions vs. follower count (>3× = signal); creator-brief acceptance rate (>40% of nervous-system-educator pitches respond positively to the brief — the brief itself is a messaging artifact).
