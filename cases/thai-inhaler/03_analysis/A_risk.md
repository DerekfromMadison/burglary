# A_risk.md — Risk / Compliance / Legal Viewpoint

**Persona:** General counsel advising a wellness CPG launch.
**Date:** 2026-04-27.
**Posture:** Conservative. FDA enforcement scrutiny is the default. The ya dom format has no scaled US precedent file the founder can point at.

---

## 1. What this viewpoint sees

A Thai herbal inhaler — a tube of menthol, camphor, eucalyptus oil, borneol, and traditional Thai botanicals presented for nasal inhalation — is the regulatory equivalent of a loaded gun pointed at the founder's bank account. The exact same physical product sold with three different labels lands in three different statutory universes:

- **"Smell our Thai blend."** → cosmetic. Lightest regulation. FD&C Act § 201(i), 21 USC § 321(i). Post-MoCRA: facility registration + product listing + safety substantiation; no premarket approval.
- **"Temporarily relieves nasal congestion."** → OTC drug. FD&C Act § 201(g). Either fits a monograph (21 CFR Part 341) or becomes an unapproved new drug.
- **"Reduces stress and anxiety."** → unapproved new drug. Disease/structure-function claim outside any monograph. This is where doTERRA, Young Living, and Quinessence got their warning letters.

The whole regulatory question reduces to: **what does the founder claim the product does?** The molecules in the tube are largely the same as Vicks Inhaler (menthol, camphor, eucalyptol). What changes legal exposure by an order of magnitude is the words on the label, the website, the TikTok video, the influencer post, the affiliate's blog, and the customer-service email template.

The FDA's own Aromatherapy guidance is explicit: "A fragrance marketed with certain 'aromatherapy' claims, such as assertions that the scent will help the consumer sleep or quit smoking, meets the definition of a drug because the intended use is to treat or prevent disease or otherwise affect the structure or functions of the human body." That sentence is the perimeter.

A second-order risk: the format — a stick held to the nose, menthol vapor inhaled — visually and functionally mimics OTC monograph drug products (Vicks Inhaler, Olbas Inhaler, Benzedrex). FDA reviewers are pattern-matchers. The format alone tilts default classification toward "drug." The founder must affirmatively pull the product back into "cosmetic" through claim language, packaging, and channel.

Format precedents:
- **Vicks Inhaler:** OTC drug under 21 CFR Part 341, active ingredient levmetamfetamine 50 mg; final monograph adopted 1994. Not a cosmetic. Monograph-compliant, no NDA.
- **Olbas Inhaler:** sold on Amazon under "aromatherapy / clear breathing" framing — walks the cosmetic line; other Olbas SKUs are OTC drugs.
- **Poy-Sian / Hong Thai:** sold on Amazon US as "menthol aromatherapy nasal inhaler," explicitly avoiding "decongestant," "relieves," and disease language; routed through cosmetic framing. (Hong Thai Lot 000332 had a microbial-contamination recall in Thailand, Dec 2024 — useful supply-chain lesson.)

## 2. Specific findings

### 2(a) Classification matrix

Class is set by **intended use** under FD&C Act § 201(g) (21 USC § 321(g)) and the FDA intended-use rule at 21 CFR § 201.128. Intended use is established not just by the label but by *all* circumstantial evidence: website, social posts, customer-service scripts, ad copy, retailer-page text, influencer briefs.

| Class | Trigger | Cost to comply | Cost to violate |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cosmetic** (§ 201(i)) | Claim limited to fragrance/mood-aroma; no body-function claim. | MoCRA registration + listing + safety file. ~$5K–$30K legal + ~$5K–$15K testing. | Misbranding; recall, seizure. Rarely criminal first instance. |
| **OTC drug, final monograph** (21 CFR Part 341) | "Temporarily relieves nasal congestion due to a cold." Active ingredient must be GRASE under monograph (e.g., menthol per § 341.80, camphor, eucalyptol). | Drug Facts panel per 21 CFR § 201.66; cGMP per Parts 210/211. Floor $50K–$200K. | Misbranded drug; warning letter, recall, injunction. |
| **OTC drug, NDA** (Part 314) | Outside monograph but branded OTC. | $1M+. Fatal for indie founder. | Plus criminal exposure under FD&C § 303. |
| **Dietary supplement** (DSHEA, § 201(ff)) | Intended for **ingestion**; structure/function claims with disclaimer per 21 CFR § 101.93. | cGMP under 21 CFR Part 111; 30-day notification. | Misbranded supplement. |
| **Unapproved new drug** | Disease/structure-function claim past the cosmetic line, no NDA. | N/A — illegal. | Warning letter is the cheap outcome; worse is seizure, injunction, criminal. This is doTERRA / Young Living / Quinessence. |
| **General consumer good** | "Air freshener," "scented decor." | CPSC, state only. | Kills the wellness pitch. |

**Critical:** a nasal inhaler is **not** a dietary supplement. DSHEA requires ingestion. Inhalation is not the statutory route. Founders sometimes paste the supplement disclaimer ("This statement has not been evaluated by the FDA…") onto an inhaler — that is misleading and signals to the FDA that the brand is making structure/function claims it knows are outside the cosmetic envelope. **Remove this option from the menu.**

Default landing zone if the founder does nothing intentional: either (a) a misbranded unapproved drug if any therapeutic-adjacent language slips through, or (b) a cosmetic, if claims stay strictly olfactory. There is no "default safe harbor."

### 2(b) Specific banned and allowed claim language

This is where most indie wellness brands die.

**Hard-prohibited drug claims** (any one triggers reclassification):
- "Relieves congestion" / "clears sinuses" / "decongestant"
- "Helps you breathe better" (FDA cited this exact pattern in Quinessence and SnoreStop)
- "Treats a cold," "fights cold and flu symptoms"
- "Relieves headaches," "stops migraines"
- "Reduces anxiety," "treats stress" (FDA's own guidance flags "treat depression or anxiety" as a drug claim)
- "Helps you sleep," "treats insomnia" (FDA-cited example)
- "Boosts immunity," "antiviral," "antimicrobial"
- Any named disease (COVID, flu, asthma, allergies, depression, ADHD)
- "Calms the nervous system" — looks soft; it's a structure/function claim about a body system, drug claim for a non-supplement
- "Boosts focus," "improves concentration" — structure/function claim; drug for an inhaler with no monograph fit
- Before/after testimonials showing symptom resolution
- Citing clinical research on menthol/eucalyptus as if it applied to the finished product

**Cosmetic-allowable language** (safer, but conservative):
- "An uplifting Thai herbal aroma"
- "A grounding scent inspired by Thai tradition"
- "Brightens your day with the scent of menthol, eucalyptus, and Thai herbs"
- "A traditional Thai sensory ritual"
- "Pocket-sized fragrance experience"
- "Refresh your senses" — borderline; safe alone, becomes risky paired with "fatigue," "energy," or "alertness"
- "A moment of calm" — borderline. "Calm" alone, framed as mood/aesthetic, has been tolerated. "Calms anxiety" is plainly a drug claim.

**Rule of thumb:** if the claim describes what the *product does to the user's body or mind*, it is a drug claim. If the claim describes what the *product smells like, evokes, references, or symbolizes*, it is cosmetic-allowable.

**Note on "aromatherapy":** the word is not banned. FDA tolerates it as a category descriptor. But aromatherapy claims are evaluated by the same drug/cosmetic test, and "aromatherapy + therapeutic claim" is the single most common warning-letter trigger.

**Operational red-line:** customer service, refund policies, FAQs, and influencer briefs must use the same dictionary as the label. The 2014 Young Living warning letter cited *distributor blog and social posts*, not the product label, as evidence of intended use. The brand is liable for what its affiliates and reps say.

### 2(c) Recent enforcement landscape — actual warning letters

**Young Living Essential Oils — September 22, 2014.** FDA cited distributor claims: "Viruses (including Ebola) are no match for Young Living Essential Oils" and "Ebola Virus cannot live in the presence of cinnamon bark (this is in Thieves) nor Oregano." Also cited: oils as cures for cancer, autism, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, MS, tetanus. Held to be unapproved drugs under § 201(g)(1)(B). 15-day response deadline. Canonical citation for "the company is liable for what its salespeople post." Young Living got a *second* warning letter June 10, 2022 (file 615777) targeting "Essential Oil," "Vitality," "Ningxia," and "Nature's Ultra CBD" lines — proof that the agency revisits repeat offenders.

**doTERRA International — September 22, 2014** (companion letter to Young Living). Same fact pattern: oil claims targeting Ebola, viruses, cancer. Same 15-day response. Companion **FTC action against doTERRA distributors landed March 2023** for COVID claims — enforcement against the affiliate channel continues a decade after the first letter, with permanent injunctions and monetary judgments. Shows that *anyone* selling the brand can be enjoined; policing only your own staff is insufficient.

**Quinessence Aromatherapy Ltd. — March 6, 2020 (file 605013).** Joint FDA/FTC letter. Cited website language including "Essential Oils to Protect Against Coronavirus" and "the most powerful anti-virus essential oils to provide defence against coronavirus" (basil, bergamot, cinnamon, clove bud, eucalyptus listed). Held to violate FD&C § 505(a) as unapproved new drugs. Lessons: (a) in a public-health crisis the agencies move within weeks, (b) FTC joins, doubling exposure, (c) being a UK company shipping to US doesn't insulate.

**Pattern:** trigger is almost always website + social copy, not the physical label. Disease names (Ebola, COVID, cancer, Alzheimer's) are guaranteed triggers. Verbs to scrub: "relieves," "treats," "heals," "fights," "kills [pathogen]," "protects against [disease]." Affiliate/distributor channels multiply enforcement risk.

### 2(d) Trademark / IP exposure

**"Ya dom" is generic.** Translates roughly as "smelling medicine"; a category descriptor, not a brand. Cannot be trademarked in the US for an inhaler — like "tea" cannot be trademarked for tea. Use it freely in descriptions; never try to own it.

**Existing US marks to clear around:** Hong Thai, Poy-Sian, Vicks (P&G — do not get clever), Olbas (Lanes Health), Tiger Balm (Haw Par), Saje, Vitruvi, Plant Therapy, doTERRA, Young Living.

**Clearance protocol:**
1. USPTO TESS knockout in **Class 003** (cosmetics, fragrance) and **Class 005** (pharmaceutical preparations). The class signals what the product is and shapes what claims are later allowable.
2. Common-law search (Google, Amazon, social handles).
3. Domain + handles availability.
4. Avoid Thai-language marks in transliteration without Thai-side IP clearance — the Thai Department of Intellectual Property has begun moving toward GI-style protections on traditional remedies.

**Trade dress:** packaging mimicking Vicks (yellow/green/red) or Hong Thai (the iconic green tube) invites opposition. Differentiate visually.

### 2(e) FTC marketing-claim risk

The FTC is the second sword. It applies to any health-related claim, regardless of FDA classification. The FTC's December 20, 2022 *Health Products Compliance Guidance* (first major update in nearly 25 years) tightened the substantiation bar:

- **Standard:** "competent and reliable scientific evidence." For health-benefit claims, this generally means **randomized, controlled human clinical testing** by qualified experts, on the *finished product as marketed*.
- **Anecdotes are not substantiation.** Influencer reviews, "I tried it and felt amazing" content, customer surveys — none of these substantiate a benefit claim.
- **Ingredient research is not product research.** Citing "menthol relieves congestion" studies does not substantiate a claim that *your* finished inhaler relieves congestion.
- **Animal/in vitro studies are insufficient on their own.**
- **Mechanism-of-action claims still need clinical proof on the product.** "Activates the parasympathetic system" is a benefit claim and needs RCT data.

**Implication:** any benefit claim — including soft-sounding ones like "supports relaxation" — needs either (a) RCTs on the finished product (six-figure cost), or (b) re-engineering to a non-benefit (sensory/aesthetic/cultural) claim. Choose (b).

**Influencer/affiliate exposure:**
- 16 CFR Part 255 (Endorsement Guides, revised 2023): material connections must be clearly disclosed.
- Brand is liable for material claims by paid creators.
- "Free product seeding" is a material connection requiring disclosure.
- 16 CFR Part 465 (FTC Rule on Fake Reviews, 2024): negative reviews cannot be suppressed; testimonials cannot be fake.

## 3. Recommendations from this lens

### Cheapest compliant launch path

**Position as a cosmetic. Do nothing else.** Do not chase OTC monograph compliance unless the strategy *requires* the right to say "relieves congestion." If it does, the brand is no longer indie wellness; it's a generic-equivalent-to-Vicks competitor and the economics change completely.

**Cosmetic launch checklist:**
1. **Claim audit & dictionary:** every word — label, packaging, web, Amazon listing, About, FAQ, return policy, customer-service templates, influencer brief, ad copy — reviewed against one consistent claim dictionary, version-controlled, signed off by counsel.
2. **MoCRA facility registration** via Cosmetics Direct (FDA portal); biennial renewal; if contract-manufactured, CMO registers and the brand is the "Responsible Person" who lists the product.
3. **MoCRA product listing** per § 364c.
4. **Safety substantiation file:** tox review of each ingredient at use level, microbial limits, stability, sensitization on finished product.
5. **Cosmetic GMP** per ISO 22716 (de facto US standard pending MoCRA cGMP rulemaking).
6. **Allergen labeling:** list common essential-oil allergens (limonene, linalool, eugenol, citral, geraniol, citronellol) above EU IFRA thresholds.
7. **California Prop 65** ingredient screen against current OEHHA list — methyleugenol and pulegone (in some essential oils/mints) are listed. Reformulate or warn. CGL policies generally do **not** cover Prop 65 absent personal injury — buy a separate endorsement.
8. **No structure/function claims; no DSHEA disclaimer.** The disclaimer is for supplements (21 CFR § 101.93). On a cosmetic it's misleading and signals intent.
9. **Trademark filing in Class 003** before any retailer pitch (Class 005 only if planning a future drug SKU).
10. **Product liability insurance:** $1M–$2M per occurrence with product-liability endorsement; aromatherapy-specific carriers exist (Alternative Balance, BBI). Confirm coverage for products *sold*, not just used in service.
11. **Influencer/affiliate template** that requires FTC disclosure, prohibits health claims, lists the approved-claim dictionary, gives the brand takedown rights, and indemnifies the brand.
12. **Customer-service script:** if asked "does this help my sinuses?" the answer is "we can't make health claims; many people enjoy it as a refreshing aromatic scent." Train. Keep transcripts.

### Specific claims to never make — prohibited-words list

`relief, relieves, treat, treats, cure, heal, prevent, decongestant, congestion, sinus, headache, migraine, anxiety, depression, panic, ADHD, insomnia, sleep aid, immunity, antiviral, antimicrobial, antibacterial, nervous system, inflammation, pain, nausea, asthma, COPD, allergies, COVID, flu, cold, virus, bacteria, infection, detox, pharmaceutical-grade, medical-grade, therapeutic-grade, doctor recommended, clinically proven`

Bake this into copy-validation before any asset publishes. Half a day of work prevents 90% of FDA exposure.

### Testing/documentation to produce

- Microbial-limits testing per batch (the Hong Thai Lot 000332 recall is exactly the failure mode).
- Stability testing (accelerated + ambient) so shelf-life claims are substantiated.
- Sensitization/patch testing on the finished product.
- Sourcing chain-of-custody (origin, supplier audit, COA per shipment, heavy-metals panel, pesticide panel).
- Allergen statement supporting documentation.
- Prop 65 screen.
- Claim-substantiation file even for cosmetic claims (perceptual panel or structured customer survey).

### When to hire counsel vs DIY

- **DIY:** prohibited-words list. Claim dictionary. MoCRA portal submissions (Cosmetics Direct is genuinely usable). Prop 65 screen.
- **Counsel for fixed-fee:** trademark search + filing in Class 003 (~$1,500–$3,000/mark). MoCRA review. Influencer/affiliate template.
- **Counsel for retainer (~$1K–$3K/mo):** ongoing claim review pre-launch; crisis response.
- **Counsel hourly:** actual FDA warning letter ($20K–$75K to respond properly). Retailer/Amazon negotiations. State AG inquiries.
- **Specifically: hire counsel before signing the manufacturing agreement.** The contract needs reps & warranties on cGMP, indemnification for ingredient failures, and a quality agreement.

### "If you only do 5 things"

1. **Write the claim dictionary and prohibited-words list. Make every brand asset comply.**
2. **Register and list with FDA under MoCRA via Cosmetics Direct.** Free. Two hours.
3. **File the trademark in Class 003 before any retailer pitch.**
4. **Buy product-liability insurance with a Prop 65 endorsement before the first unit ships.**
5. **Get a quality agreement and reps & warranties from the manufacturer covering cGMP, microbial limits, ingredient identity, indemnification.**

These five = ~80% protected. The remaining 20% is operational discipline (training staff/creators on the dictionary, batch testing, policing the affiliate channel).

## 4. What you cannot tell from this viewpoint

Risk/legal tells you legally cheap vs. expensive. It does not tell you:

- **Whether anyone wants to buy at the cosmetic-positioning level.** A pure "fragrance/vibe" pitch removes most of the "why" aromatherapy customers historically buy. The CMO and Customer viewpoints must speak to whether non-functional positioning moves units. If "no, you must claim functional benefits to sell," then the brand is structurally illegal at scale and the answer is to redesign or kill the launch.
- **Whether the cost of compliance (~$30K–$75K front-loaded) is recoverable from realistic margins and volumes.** CFO question.
- **Whether monograph compliance (going OTC drug, "relieves congestion") is economically rational.** Depends on volumes, pricing, channel, and whether retailers (CVS/Walgreens) would meaningfully upgrade shelf placement for a cold-aisle SKU. CFO/Competitor.
- **Whether the founder's identity / Thai connection mitigates appropriation criticism enough that culturally rooted positioning works.** PR/Reputation.
- **Whether the FDA will become more or less aggressive on aromatherapy in the next 3–5 years.** Futurist guess. Analysis assumes current posture continues; planning around an FDA pullback is malpractice.

I am also not opining on customs/import compliance, EU CPNP/UK/Canada notifications, or Thai-side regulatory issues at the manufacturer level — the brief specifies USA-only.

**The single most important external input I am missing: the founder's actual planned claim language.** If they intend "stress / focus / sleep" positioning — the dominant indie aromatherapy framing — this analysis collapses to one sentence: *the planned positioning is illegal and they need to either change the claim envelope or change the product.* If they intend pure sensory/cultural positioning, the cosmetic path I've outlined is workable. The bifurcation point is the founder's claim intent.
