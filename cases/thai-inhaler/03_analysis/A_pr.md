# Round A — PR / Reputation

**Persona:** Head of communications for this Thai-herbal-inhaler brand.
**Mandate:** Cultural-authenticity arc; Bloomberg-headline scenarios both ways; loudest critics; appropriation risks AND credible-carrier success patterns.

---

## 1. What this viewpoint sees — the narrative landscape

Every consumer brand that crosses a cultural border ships with a *story slot* the press will fill in for you whether you want them to or not. For a Thai herbal inhaler launched into the US wellness market, that slot has three plausible fillings, and the press picks one within roughly the first 90 seconds of touching the brand:

1. **"Daughter of Thailand brings her grandmother's 7-Eleven counter staple to LA acupuncturists."** This is the *Fly By Jing* slot, the *Omsom* slot, the *Diaspora Co.* slot. Wellness journalism most wants to write this story because it is gettable, sympathetic, has a clean hero, and lets the writer feel they are correcting a historical wrong (the appropriation of South/Southeast Asian materia medica by Goop-style wellness) by elevating a credible carrier.

2. **"White wellness founder discovers Thai pharmacy item, repackages at 12x markup, calls it 'breath ritual.'"** This is the *Lucky Lee's* slot, the *Breakfast Cure congee* slot, the *spa water* slot. Waiting for any brand whose founder narrative cannot survive ten minutes of journalist Googling. It is also the slot a *competitor* will help reporters fill, by anonymously DM'ing food and culture writers a screenshot of the founder's About page.

3. **"Nice but boring — another aromatherapy SKU."** The silent-failure slot. The brand never gets a Times Style feature, never gets a TikTok dust-up either; sells $40k a month and dies after Series Seed. Slot #2 is the active risk. Slot #3 is the passive risk.

The slot is determined fast because the press has *already cached the template*. Since 2018 — post Bon Appétit's Adam Rapoport reckoning, post Lucky Lee's, post Half-Baked Harvest's "banh mi rice bowl" pile-on, post the spa water/agua fresca discourse — wellness journalism has internalized one check: *who is the founder, who is on staff, who is in the imagery, who is paid*. Clean answers + a coherent cultural product = the Fly By Jing template ready. Dirty answers = the Lucky Lee's template ready, usually held by a freelance Substacker downstream of the staff writer.

This means the brand does not get to "manage" the narrative slot via a press kit. The slot is determined by founder biography, cap table, supplier relationship, and marketing imagery. Comms is downstream of those decisions, not upstream.

## 2. Two-arc scenario analysis

### 2a. The "credible carrier" success arc

**The Bloomberg/Times headline we want, 18 months in:**
*"This Thai-American Founder Wants To Replace Your Stress Ball With A 200-Year-Old Pharmacy Staple"* (NYT Style, *The Cut*, *Vogue Business*, *Air Mail*, *Cherry Bombe*).

**What the brand has to look like for this headline to be writable:**

- **Founder.** Legible, verifiable biographical claim, in descending power: (a) Thai or Thai-American who grew up using ya dom — analogue: Jing Gao of Fly By Jing, born Chengdu, returned to Sichuan, supperclub-to-retail; her reclaimed birth name *is* the brand. (b) Asian-American sibling co-founders framing the product as generational reclamation — analogue: the Pham sisters at Omsom, where the ethnic-aisle critique *was* the press release. (c) Non-Thai founder with a deep, long-tenured Thai co-founder on the cap table with named equity, not "supplier." Brightland is instructive: Aishwarya Iyer is Indian-American and makes California olive oil, never claiming Indian heritage. Lesson: not "don't make a Thai brand if you're not Thai" but "don't claim a heritage your cap table doesn't support."

- **Sourcing.** Single-origin, named farms, named cooperatives or family producers in Thailand. Model: Diaspora Co. — Sana Javeri Kadri's Pragati turmeric, sourced from the Kasareni family in Vijayawada, ~4x commodity price, lab-tested per harvest. Farmer photos and farm names *are* the marketing. For a Thai inhaler: a named relationship with a Thai herbalist family, regional cooperative, or small Bangkok/Chiang Mai workshop — *not* an anonymous Yiwu OEM.

- **Equity/royalty structure.** Diaspora Co.'s 4x-commodity pricing is the public-facing version. The stronger version: equity in the US entity for the Thai supplier or producing family. Disclosed, this single fact neutralizes ~80% of appropriation discourse before it starts. It is also the fact journalists most enthusiastically write about because it is novel — most CPG brands don't do it.

- **Staff and imagery.** Marketing features Thai people, Thai-language captions where natural, Thai-American creators, Thai practitioners (Thai massage therapists, Thai traditional medicine practitioners). The team page lists named Thai team members, not a Western founder with a "partner in Bangkok" footnote.

- **Claims discipline.** No "ancient Thai healing" mysticism. The product is what it *is*: a 100+ year old Thai consumer staple, a 7-Eleven counter item, a working-pharmacy product — not a sacred ritual. This protects against both FDA scrutiny and the "selling exoticism" critique. Fly By Jing's framing of Sichuan chili crisp as a daily pantry item, not exotica, is the same move.

- **Founder voice.** The founder talks about Thailand as *Thailand*, not as a "land of healing tradition." Jing Gao's framing — "changing the perception of Chinese food, one jar at a time" — is the template. The founder corrects a *misperception* (aromatherapy as lavender-coded Western essential oils), they don't introduce exotica.

**Three real-world precedents in this arc:**

1. **Fly By Jing (2018–present, Sichuan chili crisp).** Jing Gao reclaimed her birth name as the brand, ran the most successful food-product Kickstarter of its time, hit 12,000+ stores by 2024, Shake Shack partnership, James Beard for the cookbook. Biographical fit was load-bearing — nothing to apologize for to Sichuan diaspora critics.

2. **Omsom (2020–present, Asian pantry starters).** The Pham sisters launched May 2020, sold out three times, 2,000-person waitlist, covered by Vogue, NYT, CNN, Saveur. National Whole Foods 2022. The first-gen Vietnamese-American identity and the "ethnic aisle" critique *were* the brand thesis.

3. **Diaspora Co. (2017–present, single-origin Indian spices).** Sana Javeri Kadri founded at 23, launched with Pragati turmeric, grew to 150+ Indian/Sri Lankan family farm partners, ~4x commodity pricing, per-harvest lab testing. Became the press's go-to example for ethical CPG sourcing. Single-origin transparency was the moat.

A fourth, instructive case: **Tiger Balm / Haw Par.** Manufacturing kept in Singapore despite cost pressure. A century on, 100+ countries, Hollywood endorsement. Lesson: heritage products with intact provenance can age into Western markets without forfeiting authenticity — but provenance must be *real*, not narrative dressing.

### 2b. The "appropriation backlash" arc

**The headline we are trying to avoid, 6–18 months in:**
*"How A Wellness Brand Repackaged A Thai Pharmacy Staple Into A $32 'Breath Ritual' — And What Thai Diaspora Twitter Has To Say About It"* (Eater, The Cut, Refinery29, Cold Tea Collective, NextShark, Reductress for the meme cycle).

**Specific moves that trigger this arc:**

- **Founder is non-Thai with no disclosed Thai partner**, About-page copy leans on "discovered on a trip to Thailand," "ancient Thai wisdom," "elevated for the modern wellness consumer." Direct analogue: Lucky Lee's, where Arielle Haspel's "clean Chinese food" framing — explicitly contrasting against Chinese food that allegedly made her "feel bloated and icky" — read as *demeaning the source culture to elevate the product*. Direct analogue: Breakfast Cure, where Karen Taylor said her congee was "improved" for "the modern palate." The two phrasings are functionally identical and both blew up.

- **Mispronunciation, mistransliteration, misframing.** Calling it "yadom" but explaining it as "Thai aromatherapy" (it isn't, in the Western sense) or "Thai meditation tool" (it isn't) or claiming it derives from "Thai Buddhist monks" (it derives from working pharmacy practice). Direct analogue: Half-Baked Harvest's "Ginger Sesame Banh Mi Rice Bowls" — a free-floating "banh mi" attached to a dish unrelated to the actual sandwich, mispronounced, ingredients from "various Asian cultures." The criticism is not that you cooked Vietnamese food, it is that you used the *name* as a vibe-signifier.

- **No Thai people in the brand's public surface.** No Thai team, no Thai creators in marketing imagery, no Thai partners disclosed, no Thai-language presence on the site. Imagery is exclusively thin white women in beige-toned wellness lighting. This is the Goop / smudging-kits-at-Anthropologie pattern: a culturally-rooted item abstracted into aesthetic for an exclusively white, exclusively wealthy consumer surface, with the source culture invisibilized in marketing even when named in copy.

- **A "wellness" framing that makes the product sound elite.** Pricing 8–12x the Thai retail price (which is real — ya dom is ~$1 at any Bangkok 7-Eleven) without sourcing/quality justification. The criticism is two-layered: (a) you are taking a *working-class* product from another country and luxury-coding it for the West; (b) the markup is not flowing back to Thai producers.

- **Tone-deaf launch moment.** Launching during a Thai cultural-political event without acknowledgment; leveraging a Thai festival in marketing without consultation; using monks or temples in imagery without permission; using working-class Thai people as exotica.

**Who breaks the story, in order of likelihood:**

1. **A Thai-American food/culture writer** on Substack, Eater, Cold Tea Collective, or NextShark. The Lucky Lee's / Breakfast Cure pattern: a culture-specific outlet writes the first piece, it ladders up. Cold Tea Collective ran the Breakfast Cure piece; NextShark aggregates and amplifies.
2. **A Thai diaspora TikTok creator** posting a side-by-side of the brand's $32 stick next to a $1 Hong Thai or Poy Sian inhaler from a Bangkok 7-Eleven. *The most likely first trigger.* It is also the cheapest and most viral; the spa water / agua fresca cycle started this way and reached millions of views before traditional press picked it up.
3. **A competitor** quietly DMs reporters or amplifies the TikTok. The most cynical part of the arc and the part founders never see coming.
4. **A Thai-American or Thai academic** picks up the discourse on X/Bluesky and adds "I research this" credibility.
5. **A wellness-skeptic outlet** (Maintenance Phase, *Conspirituality*, *Defector*) already primed to dunk on premium-priced wellness with shaky claims. Maintenance Phase already did Moon Juice; the same template recycles.

**What the criticism looks like:**

- TikTok side-by-sides ("$32 vs $1 — same product").
- X threads cataloguing "things this brand gets wrong about Thai medicine."
- Substack longreads on the wellness industry's appetite for working-class Asian pharmacy products.
- IG comments under brand posts: deleting them gets screenshotted as receipts; leaving them becomes the visible reputation.
- A Reddit thread on r/Thailand or r/AsianAmerican that stays indexed for years.
- A *comparison piece* — "Fly By Jing did it right. [Brand] did it wrong." Worst-case, because it makes Fly By Jing the explicit *contrast class*, permanently fixing the brand in slot #2.

**Three real-world precedents in this arc:**

1. **Lucky Lee's (2019, NYC).** Arielle Haspel, white health-coach founder of a "clean" Chinese restaurant. IG copy implied existing Chinese food made her "bloated and icky." Backlash within days; *Time* ran a piece; closed within ~2 years. Cited in every appropriation-and-CPG primer since.

2. **Breakfast Cure (2021).** Karen Taylor, white Oregon founder, marketed her congee as *improved* for "your modern palate." NBC News covered the apology; Cold Tea Collective ran a long piece. Brand survived but is permanently the "what not to do" example.

3. **The "spa water" / agua fresca incident (2022, TikTok).** Gracie Norton's "anti-inflammatory spa water" videos repositioned agua fresca as a wellness discovery without attribution. Latine creators dismantled it within 72 hours; Refinery29 and Today.com covered it. Lesson: even *individuals* get pulled into this discourse — a CPG brand is a much larger target.

Adjacent precedent: **Milk Bar's "Crack Pie" (renamed 2019).** Not appropriation, but the same dynamic: one critic, Soleil Ho at the SF Chronicle, made the harm inescapable; the brand renamed. *One credible byline can force a rename, an apology, or worse*. Preemption is cheap; correction is not.

## 3. Recommendations from this lens

### Non-negotiables (founder-level, before the brand exists)

These are decisions made before naming, before logo, before website. They cannot be fixed later.

- **Founder narrative must be biographically defensible.** If the founder is Thai or Thai-American, build the brand around their face and family story (Fly By Jing, Omsom). If non-Thai, *do not* launch under a Thai-heritage frame without a named Thai co-founder on the cap table at ≥25% equity with a public-facing role. The cap table is part of the press kit whether you publish it or not — reporters infer it from the team page.
- **Sourcing must be specific, named, verifiable.** Named producers, named region, named cooperative or family, photographs of producers in the marketing (with consent and compensation), per-batch lab testing, public statement of producer pricing relative to commodity. Diaspora Co.'s 4x-commodity disclosure is the public benchmark.
- **At least one Thai person in the public-facing team.** Co-founder, head of sourcing, head of product, named advisor — not "we work with a partner in Bangkok."
- **Marketing imagery must include Thai people in non-exoticized roles.** Thai practitioners, Thai-American creators, Thai team members, Thai-language signage where natural. Not "white wellness woman holding tube against beige drape."
- **Royalty / equity to the Thai supplier or producing family.** Either equity in the US entity, or a per-unit royalty disclosed publicly. The single move that buys the most reputational capital per dollar.
- **No "ancient Thai wisdom" or "healing ritual" copy.** Frame as *normalization*, not *exoticization*. The product is a working-class Thai pharmacy staple, ~$1 in Thailand, used millions of times daily.

### The 5 specific PR moves that buy the most credibility per dollar

1. **Publish the supplier-equity / royalty structure on the site, day one.** A page called something like "How We Pay Our Producers" — named partners, photos (with consent and compensation), price-vs-commodity disclosure, equity / royalty structure. Journalists *most* want to write about this because it is novel in CPG. Cost: legal / structuring + photographer + translator. Buys: full neutralization of the primary appropriation trigger; eligibility for the Times Style "credible carrier" feature template.

2. **Seed the product to Thai-American creators *before* white wellness creators.** First 50 PR boxes go to Thai-American food, culture, and wellness creators (TikTok, Substack, IG). Their endorsement — or even non-objection — is the leading indicator that the brand is in slot #1, not slot #2. Inverse of typical wellness PR, same cost.

3. **Hire a Thai-American or Thai cultural advisor, name them publicly, pay them.** Not performative diversity hire — actual product/brand input, public bio. Their reputation is on the line with the brand, so they'll *fight* for the brand to do things right. They become the brand's first line of defense in a backlash and the first credible voice quoted by friendly press.

4. **Press-ready "in case of crisis" doc *before* launch.** Specific responses to the three most likely critique vectors: (a) "white founder, Thai product"; (b) "$1 in Thailand, $32 here"; (c) "claims sound like appropriation of traditional medicine." Each with the actual answer, the actual disclosure, the actual numbers. The crisis is faster than your reaction; the doc has to exist on day zero. Lucky Lee's, Breakfast Cure, and spa-water all moved from first criticism to mainstream press in under 96 hours.

5. **Pre-pitch the "credible carrier" feature, not the product.** Pitch to NYT Style, *The Cut*, *Air Mail*, *Vogue Business*, *Cherry Bombe*, *The Strategist*: not "new aromatherapy brand" but "this is what the Fly By Jing model looks like for Thai pharmacy products." Pre-fit the angle. The first feature in this slot does ~80% of the work for the next 12 months and makes appropriation-arc criticism harder to land because the brand is already publicly slotted in the contrast class.

### Secondary moves (still high-leverage)

- **A "Thailand at home" content thread** that openly stocks and celebrates Hong Thai, Peppermint Field, Poy Sian. They are *credibility*, not competitors. Treating them as competitors is itself the appropriation tell.
- **Refuse some opportunities.** Decline being the "wellness essentials" pick at a publication whose other picks are all $50+ white-founder brands. Over-presence in those contexts is itself a slot-#2 signal.
- **Be present in Thai-American community events** before wellness-influencer events. Low cost, enormous signal.
- **Thai-language landing page**, even with an English-only customer base. Credibility signal more than functional.

## 4. What you cannot tell from this viewpoint — epistemic humility

This viewpoint is sharply limited. It cannot answer:

- **Market size, demand, willingness-to-pay.** Whether there is a $20M-revenue business at the end of this PR strategy is invisible from here. A perfectly slot-#1 brand can still fail if there is no demand for a $32 Thai inhaler. CMO and CFO carry that.
- **Whether the founder is *actually* Thai or has a real Thai partner.** This whole analysis bifurcates on that fact, which the intake flagged as inferred. If non-Thai with no Thai partner, the recommendation is not "better PR" — it is "restructure the cap table or reposition off the Thai-heritage frame." A strategy decision, not a PR decision.
- **Whether the supplier relationship supports disclosure-and-equity.** If sourcing is anonymous Yiwu OEM, the credibility play collapses. Domain expert and Risk/Compliance know the supply chain better.
- **FDA limits on framing.** Some de-exoticization language ("used millions of times daily") may flirt with structure-function or disease-claim territory. The Risk viewpoint owns that line.
- **Competitive response.** Whether Saje, Vitruvi, or doTERRA launches a "Thai-inspired" knockoff in 90 days shapes whether the credibility moves have a window long enough to compound.
- **Long-term discourse drift.** Five years out, the appropriation frame may sharpen or loosen; the credible-carrier template may read as condescending. Futurist owns that.
- **Quantitative cost-effectiveness of the 5 moves.** Ranked by intuition from precedents. The Data analyst should treat each as a measurable hypothesis.

Shortest summary: *the brand's reputational fate is decided by founder biography and cap table before any PR person touches it*. Everything I can recommend is downstream of those facts. If the founder is Thai or Thai-American with a real supplier relationship and willingness to share equity, this brand has a Fly-By-Jing-shaped path. If not, the most honest comms recommendation is to reposition off the Thai-heritage frame entirely — slot-#2 risk is asymmetric, fast, and permanent.
