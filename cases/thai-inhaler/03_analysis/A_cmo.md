# Round A — CMO / Customer Voice

**Case**: thai-inhaler
**Lens**: CMO / Customer Voice (JTBD, segmentation, willingness-to-pay, friction)
**Author**: Round-A sub-agent
**Date**: 2026-04-27

---

## 1. What this viewpoint sees

A Thai herbal inhaler in the US is not one product. It is three different products wearing the same plastic tube, sold to three different people. The CMO lens separates the *product the founder is making* from the *jobs being hired*: customers don't buy "a Thai herbal inhaler" — they hire something to make a 10-second feeling-state shift in a context (cubicle, bathroom stall, Pilates parking lot, plane). Context is the segmentation axis, not demographics.

What this lens sees that others will miss:

- **Stated vs. revealed WTP diverge sharply.** Surveys say people will pay $10–$15 for a "natural" inhaler. Revealed data: Saje sells a $36 0.34 fl oz roll-on as their #1 best-seller; doTERRA sells $36–$88 10 mL touch roll-ons via MLM at scale ([Saje](https://www.saje.com/products/peppermint-halo-oil-blend-for-the-head-roll-on); [doTERRA](https://media.doterra.com/us/en/forms/product-guide-price-list-simplified.pdf)). The category sustains a 10–25× markup over drug-store because the Segment-1 buyer compares against $14 Erewhon smoothies, not Vicks. Pricing "premium-to-Vicks" guarantees commodity margins; pricing as sensory-wellness ritual unlocks indie-CPG margins.
- **Entry-point > demographic.** A 32-year-old woman buying for "anxiety in meetings" is a different customer from the same person buying for "post-workout headache." Different occasion, different WTP, different shelf, different creative.
- **Thai-ness is a *signifier*, not a *job*.** No US customer wakes up wanting a Thai product. They want a state-shift; Thai-ness is one of several legitimacy signals — competing with "clinical," "clean," "ancient," "biohacker-approved." Most US customers don't have a "Thai herbalism" entry point; they have "nervous-system reset," "headache without Advil," "the thing my acupuncturist gave me."

This lens picks *one* primary segment, *one* primary occasion, and a positioning that wins that occasion — resisting the temptation to launch a brand that says "Thai herbal aromatherapy" because that is not how customers shop.

## 2. Specific findings

### 2a. Three distinct US customer segments (with supporting evidence)

I'll define each by **occasion + identity** rather than by demographic alone. All three are real, observable on TikTok, in the data, and in revealed retail purchase.

**Segment 1 — "The Nervous-System Regulator" (primary beachhead)**

- Profile: women 24–40, urban/suburban, college-educated, household income $70k–$150k. Overlaps with Saje, Lululemon, Erewhon, Alo, Sakara customers. Follows nervous-system / somatic / polyvagal accounts.
- Evidence: Metropolitan Market's wellness customer is "college-educated woman aged 30+ with average income $70k–$100k+ who shops monthly" ([Beauty Independent](https://www.beautyindependent.com/former-whole-foods-buyer-turning-metropolitan-market-wellness-destination/)). McKinsey places millennial and Gen Z women as primary growth driver of the $2T global wellness market ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/consumer-packaged-goods/our-insights/future-of-wellness-trends)). Global Wellness Summit's 2026 report names "neurowellness" — products designed to *regulate the nervous system* — as a major emerging frontier ([GWS 2026](https://www.globalwellnesssummit.com/2026trends/)).
- TikTok: #nervoussystemregulation has sustained content velocity through 2025–2026 ([TikTok](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/2025-nervous-system-regulation)). Vocabulary: "co-regulate," "vagus nerve," "somatic reset," "sensory anchor."
- Why beachhead: price tolerance, existing identity-purchase behavior, and creators already producing organic content that doesn't require paid acquisition.

**Segment 2 — "The Functional User" (secondary; the 7-Eleven analogue)**

- Profile: 22–45, knowledge workers, parents, frequent travelers, immigrant Asian-Americans, ER nurses, students. Buys *for a job*, not identity. $5–$15 per unit, 2–4 in rotation.
- Evidence: this is the revealed segment for Vicks and Olbas. Walmart lists Vicks VapoInhaler 2-pack at $18.64 (~$9.32 each) ([Walmart](https://www.walmart.com/ip/Vicks-VapoInhaler-Non-Medicated-Menthol-Vapor-007-oz-Pack-of-2/110682818)). Single Olbas Inhaler at $10.03 Walmart, $8 Smallflower ([Walmart](https://www.walmart.com/ip/Olbas-Inhaler-1-Unit/26968664); [Smallflower](https://www.smallflower.com/products/olbas-inhaler)). Authentic Muay Thai Supply sells Poy-Sian Mark II singles at $3.00, 6-pack at $12.99 (~$2.17/unit), 60-piece bulk at $70.00 (~$1.17/unit) ([source](https://authenticmuaythaisupply.com/products/poy-sian-mark-ii-menthol-nasal-inhaler)).
- TikTok: the actual "ya dom" virality lives here — IShowSpeed energy moment, Bangkok Post truck-billboard, "stronger than Vicks" reactions ([Bangkok Post](https://www.tiktok.com/@bangkokpost.official/video/7484337738179792146); [The Nation](https://www.tiktok.com/@thenationthailand/video/7483737148743486740)). Driver is novelty/utility, not identity.
- Why secondary: brutal price ceiling. Winning here means $5–$10 against $1.17/unit landed-cost imports. Wrong place to start a *brand* — right place to harvest later via TikTok Shop.

**Segment 3 — "The Cultural Connoisseur / Asian-American Diaspora"**

- Profile: 1st/2nd gen Thai-Americans, broader East/SE Asian diaspora 22–45, plus non-Asian Americans with Thai-cultural affinity (Muay Thai, Thai cuisine, yoga/Buddhist practice).
- Evidence: this customer already buys Hong Thai, Poy-Sian, Peppermint Field, Siang Pure on Amazon, Etsy, and in Asian groceries. TikTok "Thai herbal inhaler explained" cluster is largely diaspora creators ([TikTok](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/thai-herbal-inhaler-yadom-explained)).
- Size & WTP: small absolute count (Thai-American ~330k per Census, broader SE-Asian-American ~5–6M) but high repeat purchase. Knows the $1–$3 reference price; won't pay $30 for a "Westernized" version of what their family buys for 30 baht at 7-Eleven.
- Why important: PR shield and credibility anchor for Segments 1 and 2. Diaspora endorsement enables Segment-1 sale without appropriation backlash; rejection produces a *Bon Appétit*-tier PR crisis.

**Two further sub-segments flagged for synthesis**: *migraine sufferers* (high WTP, FDA-claim minefield — Saje Peppermint Halo is the structure-function existence proof) and *sleep/anxiety onset* (converging with Segment 1; served by Saje Sleep Well, doTERRA Serenity, Plant Therapy Nighty Night at $11.99 — [Plant Therapy](https://www.planttherapy.com/collections/kidsafe-brand-roll-ons)).

### 2b. Jobs-to-be-done framing per segment

Following Christensen-school JTBD (functional + emotional + social job per occasion):

**Segment 1 (Nervous-System Regulator)** — "When I feel my heart rate climb in a meeting / before a hard conversation / on the subway when it's too loud, I want a quick, embodied, *legible* sensory action that signals to me and others that I'm taking care of my nervous system, so that I can re-enter the situation more present and feel like a person who has tools."
- Functional: 30-second physiological state-shift (cooling sensation, vasomotor response, breathwork prompt).
- Emotional: agency, self-care, "I have a tool," not "I need a pill."
- Social: visible-but-discreet ritual; instagrammable; signals identity affiliation with a wellness in-group.
- Hires: aromatherapy roll-ons, lavender mists, gua sha tools, breathing apps, mouth tape, ice rollers, magnesium spray, gum, vape pens (the underweighted competitor), cigarettes (the historical competitor), Xanax (the un-hirable competitor).

**Segment 2 (Functional User)** — "When I'm congested / dizzy / can't concentrate / hungover / on a long flight / studying for the bar, I want the strongest, fastest, cheapest sensory hit that clears my head, so I can keep going."
- Functional: maximum menthol/camphor punch; durability of the unit; portability.
- Emotional: novelty (especially the Thai-ness as exotic-effective signal), competence ("I have the better thing than Vicks").
- Social: minor flex around having a thing other people don't, "mini-Vicks 10×," collectible flavors.
- Hires: Vicks VapoInhaler ($9/unit), Olbas Inhaler ($8–$10), 5-Hour Energy, caffeine gum, Zyn, melatonin, Advil, energy drinks.

**Segment 3 (Diaspora / Cultural Connoisseur)** — "When I want what my mother / lola / pa carries in her purse, I want the real thing, ideally the brand my family uses, at the price my family pays, so that I can stay tethered to my heritage and not pay a Western markup for being Western."
- Functional: equivalence to the home-country reference product.
- Emotional: cultural belonging, anti-appropriation watchfulness.
- Social: gatekeeping signal (recognizing the real thing); brand-as-flag.
- Hires: Hong Thai, Poy-Sian, Peppermint Field, Siang Pure, regional store-brand from H Mart / 99 Ranch.

**Reading the table**: There are *three distinct price tiers* in the US right now for sensory aromatic SKUs. Tier 1 is drug-store ($3–$10, dominated by Vicks/Olbas/imported ya-dom). Tier 2 is mass-natural ($10–$15, dominated by Plant Therapy, Tom's, Now Foods). Tier 3 is sensory-wellness premium ($25–$40, dominated by Saje/Vitruvi/doTERRA-Touch).

Tier 4 exists for the MLM connoisseur ($50–$100, doTERRA Touch high-end), but is essentially closed to a non-MLM indie launch.

**The pricing trap**: a Thai-herbal-inhaler founder will instinctively price between Vicks and Saje — say $14–$18 — because that "feels" like a premium-natural step-up. That zone is the death zone. It is too expensive for Segment 2 (who can buy 6 Poy-Sians for $13), too cheap for Segment 1 (who reads $14 as "drugstore-tier, I doubt this works"), and *exactly* where Plant Therapy and Now Foods already live with massive Amazon SEO and decade-long brand presence.

The actionable WTP recommendation: **price at $24–$32 single SKU, $58–$78 set of three** to anchor in Tier 3. Use the Saje cognate, not the Vicks cognate.

### 2d. Friction points in current alternatives

**Friction 1 — Vicks VapoInhaler**: Smells like cough syrup. Marketed for sickness, not state-shift. Plastic feels disposable. No identity halo. Gen Z reports finding it "embarrassing" / "old-person." Product works; *brand context* is wrong for non-sick-day occasions. **Brand vacuum**, not product gap.

**Friction 2 — Olbas Inhaler**: Better aromatic profile and natural credibility, but UK-coded, herbal-musty profile skews older, dowdy packaging, no creator presence. Quiet $8 product, not a brand. The eucalyptus/wintergreen profile doesn't sing for a US Gen Z palate trained by Rhode-era fresh-clean-citrus-mint.

**Friction 3 — Saje Peppermint Halo / doTERRA Touch**: These work; Segment 1 pays $36 happily. The friction is (a) *ritual time* — 5–10 seconds to uncap, roll, recap; and (b) *visibility* — rolling oil on temples in a meeting is more committed/oily than a 1-second nasal cooling. **This is the product wedge.** The Thai-inhaler form factor is *better at the JTBD* than a roll-on — faster, drier, more discrete. Saje can't credibly launch a ya-dom equivalent without admitting its roll-on isn't ideal for the use case.

**Friction 4 — Imported ya-dom (Hong Thai, Poy-Sian, Siang Pure)**: Drugstore-tier packaging, Thai-language labels, no ingredient transparency by US standards. Distribution is Asian-grocery + Amazon long-tail + Etsy. The friction is *legitimacy mismatch with the US wellness shelf* — Erewhon/Whole Foods buyers can't place these SKUs because they don't fit the visual code. The opportunity is to be the *Fly By Jing / Omsom of ya-dom* — US-designed, shelf-ready, ingredient-transparent, sourced through Thai supply.

**Friction 5 — Hygiene**: nasal inhalers are intimate. Post-COVID hygiene fear is real and underweighted. Vicks/Olbas don't address it. A new launch needs an explicit hygiene story (sealed cap, individually wrapped, travel sleeve) that converts to creative.

## 3. Recommendations from this lens

**3a. Segmentation hypothesis** — primary beachhead is Segment 1. Segment 2 will buy on TikTok virality once the brand is hot; Segment 3 is too small to anchor on but is the PR shield. Only Segment 1 supports Tier 3 pricing, sustains organic creator content, and unlocks specialty retail (Erewhon, Free People, Credo, Goop).

**3b. Positioning angle** — "The pocket sensory reset, rooted in Thai herbalism." Hierarchy: *what it does for her* first ("a 5-second nervous-system reset, purse-sized"), *what it is* second (a Thai-style nasal inhaler), *what it's made of* third (camphor, menthol, borneol, plus 1–2 distinctive Thai herbs — kaffir lime, lemongrass, plai). Leading with "Thai herbal" reaches Segment 3 first; leading with JTBD reaches Segment 1 first and lets Segment 3 self-discover.

**3c. Pricing test (cheapest first move)** — 4-cell WTP test on the founder's landing page using Prolific/Cint traffic, $300–$600 budget. Cells: $14, $24, $32, $44. Measure revenue/visitor. Hypothesis: $24 and $32 tie for highest; $14 signals cheap; $44 deters without strong brand. Cross-validate via Van Westendorp on a separate sample. This is the cheapest invalidating experiment for the WTP question.

**3d. Creative pillars** — matched to Segment 1's day:
1. *The Reset* — 5-second use moment in friction context (before presentation, bathroom at a wedding, car after school). TikTok POV.
2. *The Roots* — cinematic short-form on Thai herbalism, founder origin, supplier village. Long-form Reels, founder-led.
3. *The Stack* — what else she keeps in her bag (mouth tape, magnesium, gua sha) — inhaler as new entrant in existing wellness-stack vocabulary. TikTok "what's in my bag."

**3e. Channel-mix bias** — DTC + TikTok Shop first, Amazon second (defensive, to capture brand search), specialty retail at month 6+ once social proof exists. Whole Foods/Erewhon buyers respond to *traction*, not decks — cheapest path to a buyer meeting is a 1M-view TikTok, not an email.

**3f. Naming** — do not lead with a Thai-language brand name (e.g., "Ya Dom"). It defaults to Segment 3 and creates pronunciation friction for Segment 1. Lead with an English brand name signaling the JTBD (state-shift, ritual, breath) and use "Thai" or a hero Thai ingredient as descriptor. Analogue: Fly By Jing (English brand, Sichuan as descriptor) vs. Lao Gan Ma (Chinese-named, primarily diaspora). Follow Fly By Jing.

**3g. Cultural-credibility carrier** — the founder's relationship to Thailand is the most leveraged asset/liability. If Thai-American or with a Thai co-founder/supplier, foreground that loudly to pre-empt appropriation discourse. If not, build an *explicit Thai partner* into the brand (named supplier, named formulator, profit-share or board seat) and communicate it upfront. Model: how Omsom centered the Pham sisters, how Brightland centered olive-grove relationships.

**3h. Sampling** — at <$1.50 landed unit cost (per the 60-pack Poy-Sian benchmark), give away ~5,000 units in months 1–3 to 200–400 creators in nervous-system, ASMR, somatic-coaching, Thai-American food, breathwork, and Pilates niches. Cost: ~$7.5k product + ~$5k fulfillment. Target: 50–100 organic posts. Materially cheaper than the $1.5k–$5k/post paid rates the same creators charge.

## 4. What this viewpoint cannot tell you

Honest epistemic limits of the CMO/Customer-Voice lens, and what other Round-A viewpoints will need to fill in:

- **Regulatory survivability.** I leaned positioning toward "nervous-system reset / state-shift," which drifts toward structure/function and disease territory ("anxiety," "headache," "calm"). Whether this survives FDA scrutiny is a Compliance question and may force retreat to purely sensory language ("aromatic ritual," "cooling sensation") that weakens the JTBD pull. Single biggest break-risk to my recommendation.
- **Unit economics.** Recommended $24–$32 without margin math. CFO must verify gross margin across DTC, Amazon (30–35% take after FBA + ads), and 50%-wholesale specialty retail, and whether CAC payback fits Segment 1 LTV.
- **Competitive counter-moves.** I treated Saje/doTERRA/Plant Therapy as static. Competitor viewpoint should war-game 30/90/365-day responses to a Saje nasal-inhaler launch in particular.
- **Cultural-authenticity depth.** Surface read only. PR/Reputation and Domain-Expert viewpoints should pressure-test the Fly By Jing analogue and the failure cases (appropriation blow-ups in turmeric/gua-sha).
- **Segment 1 sizing.** I cited McKinsey, Saje sales, TikTok velocity but didn't dollar-size the segment. Data-Analyst should propose the cheapest reach/conversion test.
- **Trend half-life.** "Nervous-system regulation" may be secular or a 2024–2026 cyclical that looks dated by 2027. Futurist should distinguish; if cyclical, brand needs exit-ramp positioning.
- **Actual customer interviews.** I'm inferring JTBD from analogues. Highest-leverage missing input: 8–12 JTBD interviews with Segment-1 women who bought Saje/doTERRA in last 90 days. No synthesis substitutes for them.
- **Skeptic's strongest objection.** I assumed the form factor is *better* for Segment 1 than a roll-on. The Skeptic should argue the opposite: nasal contact, hygiene fear, and aversion to "things in your face" may be stronger barriers than I credit — making the form factor a liability, not an asset.
- **Creative artifacts and 90-day GTM calendar** — directional only here; belongs in B and D.

This lens is strongest at *who buys this, in what occasion, against what alternatives, at what price, with what message hierarchy*. Weakest at regulatory limits, unit economics, competitor dynamics, founder fit, cultural-discourse risk, and creative artifacts.
