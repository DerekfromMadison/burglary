# D — FDA-safe claim-language enumeration

For a Thai herbal nasal inhaler (menthol/camphor/eucalyptus/borneol) targeting the "1-second daytime nervous-system reset / focus glimmer" position.

---

## 1. The classification decision tree

The active ingredients are class-agnostic — menthol is GRASE under § 341.80; camphor and eucalyptol are monograph ingredients. **The molecule does not pick the class. The claim does.**

```
[ Ingested? ]
  └─ YES → Dietary supplement (DSHEA, § 201(ff)).
           IRRELEVANT — inhalation is not ingestion.
           Do NOT use the DSHEA disclaimer; on a non-supplement
           it is misleading and signals S/F intent.
  └─ NO  → [ What does the marketing say it does? ]
           ├─ "Smells nice / pocket aromatic ritual"
           │   → COSMETIC. 21 CFR Part 700, § 201(i).
           │     MoCRA registration. No premarket review.
           ├─ "Temporarily relieves nasal congestion due to a cold"
           │   → OTC DRUG. 21 CFR Part 341 + § 201.66.
           │     Drug Facts panel; cGMP Parts 210/211.
           │     Vicks VapoInhaler precedent (levmetamfetamine 50 mg,
           │     monograph final 1994).
           ├─ "Reduces stress / boosts focus / supports nervous system"
           │   → UNAPPROVED NEW DRUG. No monograph fits.
           │     NDA economics ($1M+) = kill-switch for indie.
           │     The doTERRA / Young Living / MONQ bucket.
           └─ "Cures / treats [named disease]"
               → UNAPPROVED NEW DRUG, FAST-TRACK ENFORCEMENT.
                 Quinessence (Mar. 2020) landed in weeks during COVID.
```

**The decisive factor is the verb.** "Smells like" → cosmetic. "Relieves" → drug. "Supports" → drug (predicate is a body system, not an aesthetic).

**Format mimicry is itself evidence of intended use.** A tube held to the nose with menthol vapor inhaled looks like Vicks VapoInhaler. Per § 201.128 (final rule Aug. 2, 2021, eff. Sept. 1, 2021), intended use is established by *"expressions, the design or composition of the article, or by the circumstances surrounding the distribution of the article."* The 2021 rule expressly rejected any narrow reading limiting FDA to promotional claims — "design or composition" is an independent path to drug classification. The founder's claim language must affirmatively pull the product back into "cosmetic."

---

## 2. The "nervous-system reset / focus glimmer" position assessed

**Verdict: the *position* is survivable as a cosmetic. The *verbatim phrase* "nervous-system reset" is not.** Three failure points:

1. **"Nervous system"** — body-system referent. FDA's *Cosmetic, Drug, or Both?* lists "relax muscles, treat depression or anxiety, or help you sleep" as drug claims for "aromatherapy" essential-oil products. Structurally identical to "relax muscles" — § 201(g)(1)(C) trigger.
2. **"Reset"** implies change in user state. "Reset your morning" is poetic; "reset your nervous system" is mechanistic.
3. **"Focus"** — cognitive-performance benefit claim. FTC Dec. 20, 2022 *Health Products Compliance Guidance* requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence" (RCTs on finished product). No version of "boosts focus" is both legal and indie-affordable.

**The underlying position — daytime sensory state-shift, the cultural job of yadom — is fully survivable if re-encoded as sensory experience.** Translation:

| Concept | Drug-trigger | Cosmetic-safe |
|---|---|---|
| 1-second action | "instant nervous-system reset" | "a one-second aromatic reset" |
| Daytime alertness | "boosts focus" | "your morning glimmer" |
| Calm | "calms anxiety" | "a moment of brightness" |
| Mood shift | "elevates mood" | "an uplifting Thai aroma" |

The brand can keep the concept ("the 1-second reset") provided the noun being reset is the *moment / morning / breath*, not the *nervous system / focus / stress*. Olbas Inhaler runs this play on Amazon — *"aromatic vapors of menthol, peppermint, cajeput and eucalyptol provide an immediately refreshing sensation"* — and has held it for years. "Calm" is the live grenade: "calming aroma" is tolerated, "calms anxiety" is drug language. Allow "calm" only with sensory objects (aroma, ritual, moment), never physiological ones (nerves, stress, anxiety).

**Test for any new phrase:** does the verb act on the *product* (smells, evokes) or on the *user's body/mind* (relieves, boosts, calms)? If the verb's object is anatomical, neurological, or pathological → column C.

**MONQ (Jun. 30, 2022) is the closest format precedent.** MONQ sold a portable aromatherapy diffuser pen and was cited jointly by FDA and FTC for essential-oil claims around opioid use disorder and withdrawal. A portable inhalable-aroma product with disease/S/F claims anywhere on the website is exactly what FDA pattern-matches to enforcement.

## 4. The intended-use safe-harbor playbook

§ 201.128 intended-use is total-context. **Every owned and paid surface counts.**

**(i) Website headline.** *Allowed:* "A 1-second Thai aromatic ritual." / "The pocket aroma of Thai herbs." / "Your morning glimmer, in a tube." *Forbidden:* "Reset your nervous system in one breath." / "Boost focus naturally." / "Pocket calm." Subhead rule: an H1 cannot be neutralized by a column-C subhead — "A 1-second ritual / Reset stress between meetings" fails on "stress." About page: founder-and-Thai-tradition narrative (B_synthesis #7) is cosmetic-safe; avoid "inhalation activates the parasympathetic…" (mechanism claims trigger per FTC Dec. 2022).

**(ii) Affiliate creator briefs.** The high-leverage failure mode. Young Living's 2014 letter cited *distributor blog and social posts*, not the label. The 2023 FTC actions against doTERRA distributors (Wong, Bacot, Busch — $15K each + permanent injunction) targeted seller-run webinars. **Brand is liable for what paid creators say.** Every brief includes: column-A list, column-C banned words, 16 CFR Part 255 material-connection disclosure on every post/Story, 24-hour takedown clause, indemnification. Forbidden: before/after symptom testimonials; "I used to have anxiety/insomnia and this fixed it"; ingredient-research citations applied to the finished product (FTC 2022: ingredient research ≠ product research). Archetype risk: ASMR / sensory creators are safest — sensory vocabulary is native. Acupuncturists, nervous-system educators, biohackers are *highest-risk* — their professional vocabulary is exactly column C.

**(iii) User-generated reviews.** 16 CFR Part 465 (effective Oct. 21, 2024; up to $51,744/violation): cannot suppress negatives; cannot incentivize positive sentiment; cannot editorially feature a review containing a column-C word. A customer saying "this cured my headaches" is their speech; the brand quoting it on a PDP/ad/press kit converts it to a brand claim. **Filter reviews before promoting.** No fake/AI/insider reviews without material-connection disclosure.

**(iv) Packaging.** Front — allowed: brand name, product type ("aromatic nasal inhaler"), sensory descriptor ("Thai herbs · menthol · eucalyptus · camphor"), net contents. Front — forbidden: any column-C verb; "aromatic" is safer than "aromatherapy." Back — required (21 CFR § 701.3): INCI descending; identity statement; net quantity; Responsible Person + place of business (MoCRA term); IFRA-allergen disclosure (limonene, linalool, eugenol, citral, geraniol, citronellol). Back — forbidden: Drug Facts panel (only if monograph-compliant); DSHEA disclaimer (it's a *supplement* disclaimer; on a cosmetic it is misleading and signals S/F intent). Prop 65: screen OEHHA list (methyleugenol, pulegone). Buy the Prop 65 CGL endorsement — standard CGL excludes it absent personal injury.

## 5. MoCRA registration roadmap

MoCRA (Pub. L. 117-328, Dec. 29, 2022) is fully in force; biennial renewals running in 2026.

1. **Responsible Person** = brand owner on label. If contract-manufactured: CMO registers the *facility*; brand registers as RP and lists the *product*.
2. **Facility registration** via Cosmetics Direct, Form FDA 5066. **FDA fee: $0.** Third-party filing services charge $799–$1,449/yr (optional). Biennial renewal mandatory.
3. **Product listing (§ 364c):** per SKU, within 120 days of first US introduction.
4. **Safety file (§ 608A):** tox per ingredient; microbial limits per batch (Hong Thai Lot 000332 recall, Dec. 2024 = case study); stability; sensitization on finished product; COA, heavy-metals, pesticide per shipment.
5. **Cosmetic GMP:** ISO 22716 (de facto pending MoCRA cGMP rulemaking).
6. **AE reporting (§ 605):** records + 15-business-day FDA report on serious AEs.

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| FDA registration + listing | $0 |
| Outside-counsel claim/label/MoCRA review | $5K–$10K |
| Safety / tox file | $5K–$15K |
| Microbial + stability + sensitization testing | $5K–$10K |
| Trademark, Class 003 | $1.5K–$3K |
| Product-liability + Prop 65 endorsement, yr 1 | $2K–$5K |
| **Total** | **$18.5K–$43K** |

Aligned with A_risk's "$30K–$75K." Lower bound with a CMO running cosmetic GMP. Timeline: registration 1 hr; listing 4 hr/SKU; safety file 4–6 weeks; MoCRA-ready in 60 days.

## 6. The 1-page rule

```
==========================================================
  THAI INHALER — CLAIM DICTIONARY v1
  Cosmetic positioning under MoCRA. No drug claims, anywhere.
==========================================================

5 FOREVER SAFE
  1. "An aromatic 1-second reset"
  2. "Your morning glimmer"
  3. "An uplifting Thai herbal aroma"
  4. "A pocket-sized fragrance experience"
  5. "Inspired by ya dom, a Thai pocket ritual"

5 FOREVER TOXIC
  1. "Calms the nervous system"
  2. "Boosts focus" / "improves concentration"
  3. "Relieves congestion" / "clears sinuses"
  4. "Helps you sleep" / "reduces anxiety"
  5. "Therapeutic-grade" / "clinically proven"

META-RULE
  Look at the verb. Look at its object.
  - Verb acts on the PRODUCT (smells, evokes) → safe.
  - Verb acts on the MOMENT / SCENT / RITUAL → safe.
  - Verb acts on a BODY PART, BODY SYSTEM, EMOTION,
    or NAMED CONDITION → drug claim. STOP.

  Test: cross out the noun after the verb. If what
  remains is a body or a body-state, it's a drug claim.

  Every word your affiliate posts is YOUR word.
  Young Living 2014 and FTC v. doTERRA distributors
  2023 were lost in the affiliate channel, not on
  the label. Brief creators with this card.

  When in doubt: describe the SMELL, not the FEELING.
==========================================================
```
